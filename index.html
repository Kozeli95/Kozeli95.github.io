How to be Happy

Data science pipeline as seen through the World Happiness Report

This will be a tutorial on how to curate data, prepare it, perform exploratory data analysis, and then properly analyze said data. We will be using data revolving around happiness ratings around the world to carry out said process.

The data set we are analyzing has been curated from the Sustainable Development Solutions Networks csv regarding world happiness rankings in 2017. To learn more about this organization, you can visit their website at http://unsdsn.org/. The csv was taken from Kaggle.

The sections will be divided as:

  1. Data curation, parsing, and management

  2. Exploratory data analysis

  3. Hypothesis testing

Part 1: Data curation, parsing, and management - Data curation, parsing, and management is the first step in the data science pipeline. Since the dataset that I selected was already clean, I did not have to do much by way of tidying the data. Since this is the case, I will be dropping these columns in order to look at only the most relevant columns for this dataset. I will also be renaming the columns to names that make more sense, as their current format is a bit clunky and difficult to understand.

It seems as though every graph shows a positive relationship between their respective contributor to happiness and overall happiness. Lets find out which contributor is most closely related to related to overall happiness. We can do this by checking the correlation that each contributor has with overall happiness. Correlation is used to check how two variables vary together. If the correlation is between 0 and 1, they increase/decrease together. If the correlation is between -1 and 0, then they vary in opposite directions. The closer a correlation value is to 0, the weaker the relationship is. It is very important to remember that correlation does not imply causation, as we are simply checking the relationship between two variables, not whether one causes a change in the other.

Of every contributor to a nations overall happiness, it appears as though GDP Per Capita has the strongest, positive, linear relationship with any given nations overall happiness. While we now know that there is a positive correlation between GDP Per Capita and Happiness Score, we must now conduct a hypothesis test to see if there is indeed a relationship between the two.

Part 3: Hypothesis testing - When it comes to hypothesis testing, there are usually two hypotheses that we work with: the null hypothesis, and the alternative hypothesis. The null hypothesis generally states that there is no relationship between the two variables on which you are running the test, while the alternative hypothesis states the opposite. We will conduct a hypothesis test on GDP Per Capita to see if it is related to Happiness Score.

happy_sample <- happy_csv %>%
  sample_n(50)

sample_mean <- mean(happy_sample$`GDP Per Capita Contribution`)
sample_sd <- sd(happy_sample$`GDP Per Capita Contribution`)

pnorm(sample_mean, sample_mean/50, sample_sd, lower.tail = FALSE)
## [1] 0.01839863

Since running a hypothesis test on this data gives us a very low p-value, we reject the null hypothesis that there is no relationship between GDP Per Capita and Happiness Score. We can confidently say that the two are related.
